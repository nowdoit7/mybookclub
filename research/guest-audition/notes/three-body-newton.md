## Historical portrayal sources

- Newton Project, *General Scholium* from the 1729 English *Principia*:
  https://www.newtonproject.ox.ac.uk/view/texts/normalized/NATP00056
  - Basis used: Newton distinguishes relations inferred from phenomena from
    unsupported accounts of ultimate causes, and allows a cause to remain open
    while retaining an empirically grounded law-like relation.
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Newton's Philosophy*:
  https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/newton-philosophy/
  - Basis used: Newton's rules of reasoning address causal economy, induction,
    extrapolation beyond observed cases, and revision when new phenomena demand
    an exception or refinement.
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Newton's Principia*:
  https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/newton-principia/
  - Basis used: Newton's method limits theoretical claims to what observation
    and mathematical reasoning warrant, while permitting related causal
    questions to remain unsettled.

These ideas are paraphrased. The samples contain no historical quotation and do
not claim that Newton read this modern trilogy or held the political conclusions
written for `Reader X`.

## Limitations

- The transcript is generated book-club material, not a complete account of the
  novels or their author's intent. This case tests only the evidence already
  raised at that table.
- Newton's rules concern early-modern natural philosophy, not literary criticism,
  emergency government, or democratic accountability. Applying them to the
  discussion is an explicit imaginative extrapolation rather than historical
  reconstruction.
- The primary source is an eighteenth-century English edition, and the Stanford
  entries are modern scholarly interpretations. Together they support a narrow
  reasoning style, not a full simulation of Newton's personality, theology, or
  private beliefs.
- The blinded samples can test contribution and naturalness, but not historical
  accuracy, legal safety, or how real users respond to a famous name.
