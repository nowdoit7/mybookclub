## Portrayal research

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Blaise Pascal

URL: <https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/pascal/>

The entry documents Pascal's 1654 correspondence with Fermat on gambling
probabilities. It also describes his view that experience and reason are the
ways to learn about the natural world, while emphasizing the limits he placed
on reason in theology and moral judgment. Those strands support an imagined
reader who can structure uncertain outcomes but remains suspicious of claims
that calculation alone settles what matters.

Limitation: this is a modern scholarly synthesis, not Pascal speaking. Its
account of his theology is contested in interpretation and must not be turned
into generic skepticism, modern behavioral economics, or a claim that Pascal
held today's theory of risk.

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Pascal's Wager

URL: <https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/pascal-wager/>

The entry explains the wager as an early decision-theoretic use of possible
states, stakes, probabilities, and utilities. It also stresses that the
*Pensées* remained unfinished, that reconstructing the argument in modern
Bayesian language can be anachronistic, and that both its decision table and
maximization rule face substantial objections. This supports questions about
which outcomes and values a decision frame includes, not a stock instruction
to maximize expected value.

Limitation: a theological wager with an infinite payoff is not a general risk
management manual. The audition therefore borrows only a disciplined concern
with outcomes, stakes, and the construction of the decision frame.

### MacTutor History of Mathematics: Blaise Pascal

URL: <https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Pascal/>

The University of St Andrews biography describes Pascal and Fermat's five-letter
exchange about dice and the problem of points, including how to divide stakes
when a game stops before completion. This corroborates a documented interest
in decisions whose outcomes are unresolved rather than relying only on the
later reputation of Pascal's wager.

Limitation: this is a concise mathematical biography. It does not establish a
complete philosophy of uncertainty or license statements about how Pascal
would judge modern finance, aviation, software, or Morgan Housel's book.

## A/B construction

- The guest assignment was randomized before authoring and is recorded in the
  schema field used for later disclosure.
- Both samples use the same topic, turn count, speaker order, moderator text,
  user text, and other-reader text.
- The same analytical seat is labeled `Reader X` in both samples. Only its two
  candidate turns differ.
- Every candidate turn contains three sentences, within the product's 2–4
  sentence rule.
- The regular baseline uses a competent analytical reader focused on sampling,
  failure probability, recovery, cost, and disclosure.
- The imagined Pascal contribution asks whether missing outcomes and chosen
  values distort the decision frame before calculation begins. It responds to
  the other reader instead of lecturing or moderating.

## Safety and interpretation limits

- No sentence is presented as a Pascal quotation.
- Reader X never says that Pascal read or experienced the modern book.
- The imagined contribution does not invent meetings, experiments, biography,
  or knowledge of modern systems.
- The portrayal avoids Pascal's name, fame, and authority inside the blind
  sample, so other readers cannot submit to the celebrity rather than the idea.
- A good blind result would show only that this constrained imagined voice adds
  value to this conversation. It would not certify historical fidelity,
  generalize to other books, or justify production implementation without
  cross-book trials, red-team review, and human evaluation.
