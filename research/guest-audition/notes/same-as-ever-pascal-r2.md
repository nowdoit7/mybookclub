# Same as Ever × Blaise Pascal — Round 2 audition notes

## Purpose

This research-only Korean blind A/B case revises the imagined Pascal voice from
Round 1 without changing the underlying conversation. It does not alter the
product runtime, call a paid API, assign evaluation scores, or claim to reproduce
the historical Pascal's literal speech.

The Round 1 case remains unchanged. Round 2 preserves its fixed context, source
artifacts, allowed book evidence, baseline candidate turns, non-candidate turns,
speaker order, and turn count. A fresh coin flip placed the revised imagined
guest in `sample-b`; the identity remains hidden inside both dialogue samples as
`Reader X`.

## What changed

The first audition's interpretive move remains intact: before optimizing a safety
margin, inspect which outcomes and values the decision frame has excluded. The
new candidate turns make only a delivery change:

- acknowledge Maddie's valid concern before disagreeing;
- use contemporary conversational Korean and shorter clauses;
- make one gentle reversal or direct question per turn;
- respond to the concrete aircraft and safety-margin evidence at the table;
- avoid polished maxims, translated cadence, lectures, and repeated catchphrases.

Each candidate turn is three sentences, within the product's two-to-four sentence
rule. The candidate does not claim to have read Morgan Housel's book.

## Speech fingerprint basis

The `speechFingerprint` is a bounded writing aid, not an impersonation recipe.
Its recurring moves draw on four documented strands:

1. Pascal and Fermat's work on unresolved games of chance supports careful
   attention to possible outcomes and stakes.
2. Modern scholarship on Pascal's wager supports inspecting the construction of
   a decision frame while warning against simply translating it into modern risk
   theory.
3. Scholarship on Pascal's limits on reason supports a voice that uses
   calculation without pretending it settles every human value.
4. The Pensées manuscript commentary for fragment XXV.53 describes a persuasive
   move that begins from the valid side of an interlocutor's view and gradually
   reveals a more comprehensive side, rather than refuting them head-on.

## Limits

- The cue patterns are newly composed and are not Pascal quotations.
- The sample does not imitate seventeenth-century French, religious rhetoric, or
  the aphoristic surface of translated *Pensées* fragments.
- The historical source describes a rhetorical tendency, not a fixed verbal
  tic; therefore no cue may appear more than once in a turn and no catchphrase is
  repeated across the two candidate turns.
- A favorable blind score would support only this imagined contribution in this
  conversation. Historical review and cross-book trials would still be required
  before any product use.
