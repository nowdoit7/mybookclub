# Historical Guest Candidate Queue

## Status and boundary

This is a research queue of portrayal hypotheses, not a production guest
catalog. A genre family makes a candidate worth auditioning; it never makes the
candidate eligible for a particular title, author, character, or scene. None of
the links below authorizes invented quotations, literal claims that a historical
person read a modern book, or deference to the guest as an authority.

Only candidates with an existing frozen A/B case are ranked in Round 1. The
ranking reflects test readiness, not historical importance or production
approval.

| Round 1 rank | Candidate | Live cross-book outcome | Current status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Isaac Newton | Passed *Same as Ever*, failed *Three-Body* | Topic-sensitive; consider only when predicted to beat the regular-reader slot. |
| 2 | Blaise Pascal | Passed *Same as Ever*, language failure in English *Three-Body* | Quality leader, blocked on multilingual fingerprint design. |
| 3 | Adam Smith | Passed *Same as Ever*, failed *Three-Body* | Natural, but added less interpretation than the strong baseline. |

No listed candidate is approved for runtime. Every candidate not listed in that
table is **untested**. A candidate may move
toward runtime consideration only after blind auditions on **at least two
dissimilar books in an affinity genre plus one deliberate genre mismatch**. The
same portrayal must add book-specific interpretation in both affinity cases,
avoid leaking arguments between books, and fail safely in the mismatch. Passing
that gate still requires human source and authority-bias review.

## Queue by controlled genre family

### 1. Literary fiction

- **Primary candidate:** George Eliot — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Emotional
- **Documented lens to audition:** Sympathy as a cognitive and moral achievement,
  realism as attention to particular lives, and the way private judgment is
  corrected by consequences within a social web. This could deepen very
  different novels without reducing them to plot or biography.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The guest could become a Victorian
  moral referee, confuse Eliot with her narrators, or treat sympathy as the one
  correct response to every novel.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: George Eliot](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/george-eliot/) · [George Eliot Archive](https://georgeeliotarchive.org/)

### 2. Science fiction

- **Primary candidate:** Isaac Newton — **Live cross-test complete; not runtime-approved**
- **Category replaced:** Analytical
- **Documented lens to audition:** Separate observed relations from hypotheses
  about ultimate causes; ask how far a small body of evidence warrants a general
  law; keep a successful explanation open to contrary phenomena. The lens can
  test inference, mechanism, scale, and scientific authority across dissimilar
  science fiction.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** “Newton” may make speculative physics
  sound certified, turn every discussion into mechanics, or overwhelm other
  readers through scientific prestige.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Newton Project: General Scholium](https://www.newtonproject.ox.ac.uk/view/texts/normalized/NATP00056) · [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Newton's Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/newton-philosophy/)

### 3. Fantasy / myth

- **Primary candidate:** J. R. R. Tolkien — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Fantasy as deliberate secondary-world making,
  recovery of ordinary reality through estrangement, and consolation that does
  not erase sorrow. The audition should test whether this lens illuminates
  unfamiliar mythic and fantastical structures rather than merely comparing
  everything with Middle-earth.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** Tolkien's fame could define fantasy
  too narrowly, invite copyrighted imitation or quotations, and turn the guest
  into a genre gatekeeper.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [University of Oxford English Faculty: *The Lord of the Rings* teaching pack](https://media.podcasts.ox.ac.uk/engfac/fantasy_lit/LordoftheRingsTeachingPack.pdf) · [The Tolkien Estate: Life](https://www.tolkienestate.com/life/)

### 4. Mystery / crime

- **Primary candidate:** Cesare Beccaria — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Analytical
- **Documented lens to audition:** Distinguish proof of wrongdoing from the
  social purpose of punishment; examine proportionality, preventable harm, and
  whether institutional responses create more violence than they prevent. The
  lens could challenge both detective certainty and punishment-centered endings.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** A legal-philosophy lecture could
  displace character and form, while an imagined Beccaria might be treated as a
  modern criminologist or final judge of justice.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [U.S. Office of Justice Programs: *On Crimes and Punishments*](https://www.ojp.gov/ncjrs/virtual-library/abstracts/crimes-and-punishments) · [Online Library of Liberty: *An Essay on Crimes and Punishments*](https://oll.libertyfund.org/titles/beccaria-an-essay-on-crimes-and-punishments)

### 5. Romance

- **Primary candidate:** Jane Austen — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Read courtship as an interaction among
  affection, money, law, rank, self-knowledge, and irony. This can make romance
  choices materially specific without dismissing the emotional stakes.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The portrayal may become a stream of
  faux-Austen witticisms, universalize Regency marriage constraints, or judge all
  romance by one canon.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Jane Austen's Fiction Manuscripts Digital Edition](https://janeausten.ac.uk/) · [Jane Austen Society of North America: The Marriage Law of Jane Austen's World](https://jasna.org/publications-2/persuasions-online/vol36no1/bailey/)

### 6. Horror / gothic

- **Primary candidate:** Mary Shelley — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Emotional
- **Documented lens to audition:** Ask what creators owe what they bring into the
  world, how abandonment and social rejection help manufacture monstrosity, and
  where ambition evades care. The lens should be tested on gothic and horror
  books with no creator-creature plot.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The guest could overfit every book to
  *Frankenstein*, invent autobiographical explanations, or make one famous work
  the authority on an entire genre.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Shelley-Godwin Archive: *Frankenstein* manuscripts](https://shelleygodwinarchive.org/contents/frankenstein/) · [U.S. National Endowment for the Humanities: Shelley-Godwin Archive](https://www.neh.gov/explore/the-shelley-godwin-archive)

### 7. History / politics

- **Primary candidate:** Thucydides — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Separate publicly stated grievances from
  underlying causes, test conflicting eyewitness accounts, and examine how fear,
  honor, interest, power, and rhetoric reshape collective decisions. The lens can
  pressure both narrative history and political argument.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** “Thucydidean” realism may be presented
  as a timeless law, normalize power politics, or erase people outside elite and
  military records.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [MIT Internet Classics Archive: *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Book I](https://classics.mit.edu/Thucydides/pelopwar.1.first.html) · [Encyclopaedia Britannica: Thucydides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thucydides-Greek-historian)

### 8. Science / nature

- **Primary candidate:** Alexander von Humboldt — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Observe nature across connected scales rather
  than isolating a specimen from climate, geography, culture, and human action.
  This could connect scientific evidence with ecological and social consequences
  across very different nature writing.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The imagined explorer may romanticize
  expedition science, overlook colonial extraction, or turn interconnectedness
  into a vague answer that fits every book.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Smithsonian Institution: Who Was Alexander von Humboldt?](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smithsonian-institution/who-was-alexander-von-humboldt-180974473/) · [Smithsonian Institution: Humboldt's influence](https://www.si.edu/support/impact/humboldt)

### 9. Philosophy / religion

- **Primary candidate:** Blaise Pascal — **Live cross-test complete; multilingual redesign required**
- **Category replaced:** Analytical
- **Documented lens to audition:** Combine disciplined reasoning under
  uncertainty with suspicion of self-flattering frames, and mark where reason
  can compare choices without settling the values or ultimate commitments at
  stake. The candidate must not reduce every book to the wager.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** Pascal's Wager can become a repetitive
  trick, and the portrayal can smuggle apologetics or seventeenth-century claims
  into the conversation as neutral logic.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Blaise Pascal](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/pascal/) · [MacTutor History of Mathematics: Blaise Pascal](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Pascal/)

### 10. Psychology / self-help

- **Primary candidate:** William James — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Analytical
- **Documented lens to audition:** Treat attention, habit, will, lived experience,
  and practical consequences as connected but not identical; ask what a stated
  belief changes in conduct. This can challenge self-help promises without
  diagnosing the author or reader.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The guest may sound like a modern
  therapist, turn pragmatic inquiry into “whatever works,” or lend scientific
  authority to unsupported personal advice.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: William James](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/james/) · [Harvard Library: Modern History of Science Collections](https://library.harvard.edu/collections/modern-history-science-collections)

### 11. Business / economics

- **Primary candidate:** Adam Smith — **Live cross-test complete; not runtime-approved**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Put prudence beside justice and sympathy;
  inspect decisions from an impartial spectator's position; distinguish private
  advantage from institutional rules that expose others to harm. This resists the
  caricature of Smith as a slogan for self-interest.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The famous name may settle economic
  disputes, collapse into “the invisible hand,” or falsely translate
  eighteenth-century moral philosophy into complete modern policy advice.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [University of Glasgow: *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*](https://www.gla.ac.uk/explore/adamsmith300/explorelearn/ideas/keyworks/theoryofmoralsentiments/) · [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Smith's Moral and Political Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/smith-moral-political/)

### 12. Biography / memoir

- **Primary candidate:** Plutarch — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Contextual
- **Documented lens to audition:** Read a life through revealing choices and
  small incidents, then test character by comparison with a genuinely different
  life. The paired method could expose what a memoir's self-narration selects or
  omits without pretending anecdotes are neutral facts.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** Moral comparison can flatten social
  conditions, privilege elite “great lives,” and repeat memorable anecdotes as
  verified history.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Plutarch](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plutarch/) · [Perseus Digital Library: Plutarch and the *Parallel Lives*](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.04.0104%3Aentry%3Dplutarchus-bio-1)

### 13. Poetry / drama

- **Primary candidate:** Emily Dickinson — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Emotional
- **Documented lens to audition:** Attend to compression, silence, punctuation,
  variant word choices, and the instability of apparently simple definitions of
  death, time, faith, or self. The guest should help the table stay with
  ambiguity rather than produce imitation poems.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** Generated speech could become fake
  Dickinson verse or quotations, while the famous compressed style could make a
  recognizable costume substitute for discussion.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [Harvard Library: The Emily Dickinson Collection](https://library.harvard.edu/collections/emily-dickinson-collection) · [Poetry Foundation: Emily Dickinson](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/emily-dickinson)

### 14. Children / young adult

- **Primary candidate:** Hans Christian Andersen — **Untested**
- **Category replaced:** Emotional
- **Documented lens to audition:** Preserve the double address to young and adult
  readers, attend to social exclusion and transformation, and resist turning
  pain into a simple lesson. The lens could test whether a book respects a young
  reader's complexity rather than merely rewarding good behavior.
- **Largest portrayal / authority risk:** The portrayal may import softened
  adaptations, perform a falsely childish voice, or treat nineteenth-century
  moral and religious assumptions as universal childhood truths.
- **Portrayal-review sources:** [University of Southern Denmark: Hans Christian Andersen biography](https://andersen.sdu.dk/liv/biografi/index_e.html) · [Royal Danish Library: The fairy-tale author Hans Christian Andersen](https://www.kb.dk/en/inspiration/book-festival-royal-danish-library/fairy-tale-author-hans-christian-andersen)

## What is intentionally absent

- No exact-title, author, character, or scene allowlist.
- No claim that one candidate owns a genre; the queue is designed to grow only
  after comparative auditions.
- No generated historical quotations, voice imitation, or claim of literal
  modern-book experience.
- No runtime selection, portrait, prompt, or SPEC change on the strength of this
  document alone.
